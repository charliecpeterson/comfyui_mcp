# Enrichment Palette — Krea 2

Open this in **Enhance Mode** (thin seed, or an improve-this-draft request). It's the menu you pick from. Pick by scene-type, not by working down the list — most prompts use 4–6 enrichments total, drawn from 3–4 categories. Krea 2's defining lever is **named surfaces + named light**, so categories 2 and 3 carry the most weight here.

## The off-center detail rule (master rule)

Every Enhance Mode output MUST contain exactly ONE small, slightly unexpected, hyper-specific detail. This is the single biggest anti-generic move — without it, even a richly enriched prompt regresses toward the stock-photo mean.

Examples:
- A coffee ring on the upper-left of the placemat from the cup before this one
- One sleeve rolled higher than the other
- A band-aid on the left knuckle
- A bobby pin tucked behind one ear, holding nothing in place
- A receipt face-up showing a single line item
- A hairline of rust bleeding from one bolt
- A single pine needle caught in the braid
- One shoelace tied tighter than the other
- A faded hand-stamp on the back of one hand from last night

The detail should feel **observed**, not invented — something a documentary photographer would notice across the room. If it reads as a prompt-engineering trick, drop it. **One per prompt. Never zero. Never two** (two cancel out; the eye reads them as styling).

## Scene type → which categories to lean on

| Scene type | Lean heavily on |
|---|---|
| Single-subject portrait | 1 Subject specifics, 4 Wardrobe/accessories, 3 Light, named anchor |
| Character in environment | 1 Subject (light), 2 Surfaces, 3 Light, 5 Environmental props |
| Environment / architecture | 2 Surfaces, 3 Light, 6 Weather/era, one human trace |
| Product / object | 2 Surfaces (material), 3 Light, surface staging, named anchor |
| Poster / sign / text artifact | 2 Surfaces (paper/ink), text specificity, 3 raking light, era anchor |
| Photoreal / "raw" look | 2 Surfaces, 3 Light, 7 Raw-photographic conditions |
| Abstract / surreal | **Skip the rubric.** Let the seed lead. Add light + one off-center detail + at most one named anchor. |

## 1. Subject specifics (face / skin / hair / hands)

1–3 on any human subject. AI defaults to symmetrical and unblemished; specificity here is the difference between "doll" and "person."

- **Face:** faint asymmetry; a chipped front tooth visible when smiling; a small scar through one brow; a beauty mark below the lip; smile lines; a sunburn line where sunglasses sat.
- **Skin:** freckles across the nose / scattered on the shoulders; visible pores on the cheekbones in close-up; a faint sheen of sweat at the hairline or collarbone; a tan line at the sleeve or collar; a healing scrape on the knee.
- **Hair:** a flyaway strand across the eye; tucked behind one ear, the other loose; slept-on and faintly disheveled; recently cut, slightly uneven; wet and still drying; a single grey hair.
- **Hands:** calloused palms (name the cause: guitar strings, rope, weights); ink- or clay-stained; a band-aid on one knuckle; a pale tan line from a removed watch; chipped nail polish (name the color). *Keep hands in simple, single poses — anatomy is Krea's soft spot.*

## 2. Material / surface language (Krea's master lever)

The highest-leverage category for Krea 2. Replace generic nouns with named materials and their condition. Krea renders a **named** surface far better than a described "texture."

- **Metal:** brushed steel, hammered copper, oxidized brass, galvanized tin, chrome with fingerprint smudges, rusted iron with flaking paint.
- **Wood:** sun-bleached pine, oiled walnut, weathered teak, splintered plywood, varnish crazed with age.
- **Fabric:** raw linen, brushed wool, waxed canvas, cracked leather, ribbed corduroy, sheer organza, a snagged thread at the cuff.
- **Stone / ground:** wet asphalt, mossy granite, cracked terracotta, polished concrete with hairline fractures, gravel still dark from rain.
- **Glass / ceramic:** chipped enamel, crazed porcelain glaze, condensation-fogged glass, a hairline crack in a tumbler.
- **Paper / ink (for text artifacts):** deckled cream stock, ink-rich indigo lettering, halftone dots, registration drift, a creased corner, absorption bleed at the letter edges.

Name the material, then name its condition (chipped, oxidized, sun-bleached, fogged, frayed). That pairing is what reads as real.

## 3. Light interaction (always 2–3 details)

Don't say "golden hour" alone — say what it *does*. Krea renders named light far better than "beautiful lighting."

- **Quality:** soft window light; hard golden-hour sun; overcast softbox flat; sodium-vapor street orange; candlelit underglow; blue-hour ambient.
- **Direction:** low rim from behind; raking side-light across a textured surface; top-down; underlit; backlit with the subject in near-silhouette.
- **Interaction (the part that matters):** dust or pollen drifting in the beam; steam crossing through a sunbeam; long shadows stretching across the floor; a thin rim separating shoulder from a dark ground; specular glints walking across wet asphalt; bounce fill softening the shadow side.

Pick a quality + a direction + one interaction. That trio is the whole game.

## 4. Wardrobe & accessories

Specific beats abundant. One well-described garment with a condition beats a costume list.

- Garment + material + condition: "a pale raw-linen shirt, cuffs rolled twice, a faint coffee stain near the second button."
- Accessories with wear: "a cracked leather watch strap," "wire-frame glasses with one arm slightly bent," "a canvas tote gone soft at the corners."
- Footwear with use: "scuffed work boots, one lace shorter than the other."

## 5. Environmental props (1–2)

A prop that implies the subject was here before the camera arrived. "A half-finished mug of tea cooling on the sill," "a paperback face-down with the spine creased flat," "a bicycle leaned against the wall with one pedal up." These ground the scene and often double as the off-center detail.

## 6. Weather, era, and one human trace

For environments and architecture: name the weather as a surface condition ("pavement still dark and reflective after rain," "frost feathering the lower corner of the window"), name the era through materials rather than labels ("pre-war tile, brass fittings gone dull"), and add a single human trace ("a coat left over one chair back," "fresh footprints in the dust toward the far door").

## 7. Raw-photographic conditions (for photoreal)

Krea's signature is a "raw" look, not clean AI gloss. Name the photographic conditions:
- "shot on color negative film, slight grain, low contrast"
- "available light only, mild underexposure held"
- "handheld with a touch of motion blur"
- "overcast flat light, muted palette, low dynamic range"
- "85mm at f/2, gentle falloff, natural skin texture"

These pull the output toward a believable photograph and away from over-rendered perfection.

## 8. Named-reference anchors (use at most ONE)

A real visual register gives Krea something concrete. Use one, never stacked, and never as a substitute for named surfaces/light.

- **Photographers / DOPs:** Saul Leiter (color, glass, reflection), Petra Collins (soft editorial), Gregory Crewdson (staged suburban tableau), Roger Deakins (cinematic naturalism), Fan Ho (geometric light-and-shadow).
- **Films / looks:** the muted naturalism of a slow European drama; the sodium-and-neon of a rain-soaked noir; the bleached daylight of a desert western.
- **Art movements / media:** mid-century screenprint poster; loose gouache illustration; Dutch-master still-life chiaroscuro; 1990s anime cel.

Anchor to a register, never to "cinematic" / "professional" / "atmospheric" alone — those are slop tokens, not anchors.
