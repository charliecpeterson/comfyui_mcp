# Cinematography & Photography Reference

Flux is unusually responsive to real filmmaking and photography vocabulary because its training data includes a lot of professionally-tagged photographic and cinematic content. Using the actual terms a cinematographer or photographer would use gives you precise control.

This file covers: lenses & focal lengths, film stocks & looks, lighting setups, named photographer/cinematographer styles, and color grading vocabulary.

---

## Lens / focal length

| Focal length | Look | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| 14–24mm (ultra-wide) | Distorted edges, exaggerated depth, foreground feels close, background feels far | Architectural interiors, dramatic landscapes, "alone in a vast space" |
| 24–35mm (wide) | Slight perspective stretch, environmental context, documentary feel | Environmental portraits, candid scenes, street photography |
| 35mm | Most "documentary" — what the eye sees with slight expansion | Reportage, candid storytelling, naturalistic framing |
| 50mm | Natural — closest to human eye perspective | Standard portrait, naturalistic scenes |
| 85mm | Portrait compression, flattering facial proportions, smooth bokeh | Beauty/fashion portraits, isolated subject against blurred background |
| 100–135mm | Strong subject isolation, compressed planes, long-lens look | Detail shots, stalking-the-subject feel, distant intimacy |
| 200mm+ (telephoto) | Heavily compressed, flattens depth | Wildlife, sports, "stolen moment" feel |

**Aperture (f-number):** lower = shallower depth of field.
- **f/1.2–f/1.8:** extremely shallow, dreamy, only subject's eyes might be in focus
- **f/2–f/2.8:** classical portrait depth, subject sharp, background lovely bokeh
- **f/4–f/5.6:** environmental subject + somewhat soft background
- **f/8–f/11:** deep focus, landscape standard, everything sharp
- **f/16+:** extreme deep focus, often used with movement or environmental work

## Film stocks (named looks)

Naming a real film stock gives Flux a recognizable color/grain signature. Pick the one whose mood matches the scene.

**Color negative film:**
- **Kodak Portra 400** — warm skin tones, soft contrast, fashion/portrait standard
- **Kodak Portra 800** — pushed Portra look, slightly more grain
- **Kodak Ektar 100** — saturated, fine grain, landscape favorite
- **Kodak Gold 200** — warm consumer color, vintage family-album feel
- **Fuji Pro 400H** — cool tones, soft pastels, wedding photography
- **Fuji Velvia 50** — extremely saturated, vivid greens and reds, landscape signature
- **Cinestill 800T** — tungsten-balanced, signature red halos around bright lights, neon-night look

**Black and white:**
- **Kodak Tri-X 400** — classic documentary B&W, visible grain, high contrast
- **Ilford HP5 Plus** — slightly softer than Tri-X, gritty street look
- **Ilford Delta 3200** — heavily grained, available-light night photography
- **Kodak T-Max 100** — fine grain, smooth tones, fine-art B&W

**Cinema stocks:**
- **Kodak Vision3 250D** — daylight cinema standard, neutral
- **Kodak Vision3 500T** — tungsten cinema standard, low-light cinema
- **Fuji Eterna** — muted, low-contrast cinema look (now discontinued but still recognized)

**Slide / transparency:**
- **Kodachrome** — discontinued but iconic; warm, saturated, mid-20th-century National Geographic look

## Lighting setups (named professional patterns)

| Name | Look | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Three-point lighting** | Commercial, clean | Key (main) + fill (shadow softener) + rim (separation from background) |
| **Rembrandt lighting** | Painterly, classical portrait | Single key at 45° creating a small triangle of light on the shadowed cheek |
| **Chiaroscuro** | High contrast, dramatic | Strong directional key, no fill, deep shadows preserved |
| **Butterfly lighting** | Beauty/glamour | Light directly above the camera, creating a small shadow under the nose |
| **Split lighting** | Tension, drama | Key 90° to one side, half the face in shadow |
| **Loop lighting** | Standard portrait | Key slightly above and to one side, small loop-shadow from the nose |
| **Top-down / overhead noon sun** | Harsh, exposed | Sun directly above, deep eye sockets and chin shadows |
| **Practical lighting** | Realistic, naturalistic | Lit by visible in-scene sources (lamps, neon, candles, windows) |
| **Available light** | Documentary | No added light, whatever's there is the only light |
| **High-key lighting** | Bright, low-contrast | Mostly fill light, minimal shadow, optimistic mood |
| **Low-key lighting** | Dark, high-contrast | Most of frame in shadow, isolated highlights, noir mood |
| **Backlighting / rim lighting** | Cinematic separation | Light from behind subject creates a glowing outline |
| **Window light** | Soft, natural | Diffused light from a single window source |
| **Golden hour** | Warm, low-angle | First hour after sunrise / last hour before sunset, warm side-light |
| **Blue hour** | Cool, moody | Twilight between sunset and dark, soft blue ambient + warm artificial |

## Named photographer / cinematographer styles

Flux recognizes these reference points well. They're shorthand for whole visual vocabularies.

**Cinematographers:**
- **Roger Deakins** (Coen Brothers, Blade Runner 2049) — naturalistic, single-source lighting, restrained palettes, lots of negative space
- **Emmanuel Lubezki** (Malick, Iñárritu) — handheld, natural light, magic-hour bias, immersive
- **Bradford Young** (Arrival, Selma) — low-light, color-rich, brown-skin-respectful lighting, soft shadows
- **Hoyte van Hoytema** (Interstellar, Nope) — 65mm, large-format, deep depth, monumental
- **Christopher Doyle** (Wong Kar-wai) — saturated jewel tones, neon, handheld intimacy
- **Robert Yeoman** (Wes Anderson) — symmetrical, pastel, flat lighting, centered subjects
- **Vittorio Storaro** (Apocalypse Now, The Conformist) — color-symbolic, geometric light shaping
- **Gordon Willis** (The Godfather) — top-down dark lighting, eyes in shadow, deep amber

**Photographers:**
- **Annie Leibovitz** — staged-but-intimate environmental portraits, theatrical
- **Nan Goldin** — flash-lit intimate documentary, available light at night
- **Wolfgang Tillmans** — casual snapshot intimacy, mundane subjects beautifully framed
- **Petra Collins** — soft pink/cyan grading, dreamy young-woman portraiture, film grain
- **Gregory Crewdson** — large-format suburban tableaux, hyperreal cinema-lit
- **Saul Leiter** — color street photography, reflection/refraction, foreground obstructions
- **Vivian Maier** — mid-century street photography, candid B&W, urban intimacy
- **Sebastião Salgado** — B&W reportage, monumental humanism, deep contrast
- **William Eggleston** — color-saturated American mundane, snapshot democracy

**Art / illustration anchors:**
- **Caravaggio** — extreme chiaroscuro, dramatic single-source light from upper left
- **Vermeer** — soft window light from the left, quiet domestic interiors
- **Edward Hopper** — flat afternoon light, urban loneliness, hard-edged color blocks
- **Studio Ghibli backgrounds** — painterly skies, hand-painted detail in foliage, soft warm color
- **Moebius / Jean Giraud** — clean line, flat color, mid-distance sci-fi/fantasy framing
- **Norman Rockwell** — warm, narrative, gentle American midcentury
- **Eyvind Earle** (Sleeping Beauty backgrounds) — stylized, layered, jewel-tone medieval

## Color grading vocabulary

| Term | Look |
|---|---|
| **Teal and orange** | Hollywood blockbuster default — skin pushed orange, shadows pushed teal |
| **Bleach bypass** | Desaturated highlights, retained silver in shadows, Saving-Private-Ryan look |
| **Cross-processed** | Slide film developed in negative chemistry, magenta/green color shifts |
| **Day-for-night** | Underexposed and blue-graded daytime to simulate moonlight |
| **Faded film** | Slightly washed contrast, lifted blacks, vintage feel |
| **Push-processed** | Higher grain, increased contrast, journalistic feel |
| **Muted palette** | Limited color range, predominantly one hue family |
| **Two-tone** | Two dominant complementary colors (pink/cyan, amber/blue, etc.) |
| **Monochrome with one color** | B&W scene with a single colored element preserved |
| **Pastel grading** | Pushed-up midtones, low saturation, dreamy |
| **High-contrast B&W** | Deep shadows, bright highlights, midtones compressed |
| **Soft contrast** | Lifted blacks, gentle tonal range, Portra/wedding-photo register |

## How to combine these

A good "camera and light" addition to a Flux prompt is typically:

> *[Style anchor], shot on [lens / format] at [aperture], [lighting setup] from [direction], [color grade or grain note].*

Example:
> *Documentary photography in the style of Saul Leiter, shot on a 50mm at f/2.8, available light through a rain-streaked window, slightly faded color with greens pushed and reds desaturated.*

You don't need all of these in one prompt. Pick the two or three that the scene actually needs. Most prompts only need: **lighting direction + style anchor + maybe a lens.**
